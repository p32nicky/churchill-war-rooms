The Site

An Underground
Time Capsule

A week before war was declared in 1939, Britain's government went underground — into a reinforced basement beneath the Treasury that became the secret nerve-centre of the entire war.

From this warren of cramped, map-lined rooms, Churchill broadcast to the nation, took scrambled calls with President Roosevelt on a secret hotline hidden in a room disguised as his private toilet, and chaired the meetings that decided the war's direction.

When peace came in 1945, the staff switched off the lights and walked out. The rooms sat sealed for decades. Today every detail survives — preserved alongside the immersive Churchill Museum, the definitive collection on the man himself.

Dossier · What's Down There

Five Rooms
History Held Its Breath In

01
The Map Room
Manned every minute of the war. The original wall maps are still studded with the pinholes that tracked convoys across the Atlantic, night after night.
02
The Cabinet War Room
Where Churchill chaired more than 100 wartime cabinet meetings. His chair still sits at the head of the table — its arms scored by his ring during the tensest debates.
03
Churchill's Bedroom
The PM's private quarters: his bed, desk, and the BBC microphone he used to broadcast several wartime addresses to the nation.
04
The Transatlantic Hotline
Disguised as Churchill's private toilet, this closet hid the scrambled telephone that connected the bunker directly to the White House.
05
The Churchill Museum
An award-winning, interactive museum tracing his entire life — soldier, writer, statesman, wartime leader — through speeches, letters and personal effects.
Authentic · Not a Reconstruction

Frozen
Since 1945

Almost nothing here is a replica. These are the actual desks, telephones, typewriters and bunk beds used by the hundreds of staff who lived underground — sometimes for weeks without daylight.

A free audio guide walks you room by room with first-hand accounts from the people who were there. There's no time limit once you're inside.
